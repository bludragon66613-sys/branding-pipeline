The property that
built a philosophy.
In 1988, Adrian Zecha opened Amanpuri — his first. Forty pavilions on a hillside above the Andaman Sea, set into a coconut grove. The pool is black. The roofs are Thai temple-pitched. There is no brand signage anywhere on the property. Guests arrive by longtail boat, not lobby. Zecha's rule: the property must cost you something to reach — not money, effort. That effort is part of the purification. The silence at Amanpuri is not an absence. It is a presence — composed, deliberate, earned. Never more than forty keys. Never a conference centre. Never a spa that tries to be bigger than the landscape.
The Translation
"Silence is not what is missing. It is the thing itself — fully present."
Sérac does not replicate Aman's pavilion form or its Thai vernacular — the climate, the site, the culture are entirely different. It inherits the method: that quietness is a design decision, not a default. That a property of twelve homes should feel smaller than it is. That no element should announce itself. Twelve villas in Jubilee Hills, designed around the silence that good addresses already carry.
01
Pavilion
threshold
Each villa entry is understood as a transition — not a door but a sequence. A covered threshold between the motor court and the living volume, where the outside world is formally left behind. The roof lowers before it opens. Arrival is slowed by design.
02
Black water
pool
The private pool at each villa is finished in dark granite aggregate — the water reads as a mirror of the sky, not a volume of water. At dusk it disappears into the garden. At noon it is the most still object on the property. Nothing about it is decorative.
03
Grove
canopy
The existing canopy at Prashashan Nagar is not a landscaping decision — it is a decades-old fact of the site. The villas are positioned around it, not planted around them. Every mature tree is treated as a structural element: load-bearing for the atmosphere, non-negotiable.
04
Silence
protocol
Twelve homes share one gated entry. No shared facilities, no common corridors — each villa is entirely private, entirely independent. The estate's silence comes not from soundproofing but from having twelve residents where a lesser project would have placed two hundred.
"Nothing on the property competes for your attention."
Sérac's design grammar begins here: every element resolved before it is placed, so that the guest never has to resolve it themselves. The result is a property that feels inevitable — as though it could not have been otherwise.

The Aman Standard
What
appropriate
means.
The word luxury arrives carrying obligations — to impress, to exceed, to perform. Zecha refused it. He chose a different word: appropriate. Appropriate to the site. Appropriate to the guest. Appropriate to what this particular hillside in this particular country already was. Sérac is briefed the same way: appropriate to Jubilee Hills, to the twelve families who will live here, to the silence that the address has already earned.
"
We never use the word luxury. We use the word appropriate.
— Adrian Zecha, founder, Aman Resorts · 1988
Sérac is designed from the same logic: nothing is added that should not be there. Nothing is removed that belongs.

Prashashan Nagar occupies a peculiar position in Hyderabad: it is one of the few addresses in the city that stopped needing to announce itself. The canopy filled in over decades without a landscape architect's instruction. The families who live here long since stopped discussing the postcode. There is no through traffic — which in Hyderabad in 2026 is not a feature, it is an inheritance. Sérac is built inside that inheritance. The architecture does not try to stand out from the neighbourhood. It tries to be the neighbourhood's most resolved expression.
The Developer

Thirty-five years building the commercial infrastructure of a city that never stopped growing. Over ten million square feet of premium commercial real estate in Hyderabad — privately held, privately financed, delivered without press releases. SSquare has never needed to market its standard of work. The buildings are the argument, and Hyderabad knows them.

The same material discipline and long-horizon thinking that built their commercial portfolio, applied — for the first time — to the question of where twelve families will actually live. Not a pivot. A single, deliberate extension of the same commitment.

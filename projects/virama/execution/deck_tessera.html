Ravenna, Italy · 430 – 547 AD
The gold that
changes all day.
At Ravenna in the 5th and 6th centuries, Byzantine craftsmen built the greatest mosaics in the world. In the Mausoleum of Galla Placidia, 430 AD. In the Basilica of San Vitale, 547 AD. They used individual tesserae — small pieces of glass, stone, and gold leaf — to create images of impossible luminosity.

The gold tesserae were set at a slight angle, deliberately. Each tile faces a different direction, so that throughout the day — from dawn to noon to dusk — the same surface catches light differently. The mosaic at dawn is not the same mosaic at noon. It is the same twelve hundred tiles, reading differently in different light.
The Translation
"A great estate is not a single object. It is twelve individual residences that together compose something complete."
Bvlgari's Serpenti jewellery uses the same logic at scale: individual scales forming a continuous sinuous line, each scale catching light at its own angle. The whole is only legible because each piece is distinct. Tessera is the same argument applied to residential architecture: twelve villas, each differently oriented, each catching its own light — and together, a complete world.
01
Gold tessera
panel
The entrance hall in each villa features a mosaic wall panel — hand-set gold and amber tesserae, each tile individually angled. No two installations are identical. Like the Ravenna originals, the panel reads differently at different times of day. This is the first thing a guest sees.
02
Lapis
threshold
The entry threshold and primary floor panels in deep lapis-blue stone — the same mineral the Byzantine craftsmen ground to make ultramarine paint. At Tessera it is set in geometric patterns: a floor that declares its lineage without requiring explanation. Cold to the foot. Warm to the eye.
03
Byzantine
geometry
Every window opening, archway, and room transition is governed by Byzantine geometric proportions — the ratio systems developed in the 5th century to produce spaces that feel larger than their measurements. Not ornament. Structural logic that manifests as spatial generosity.
04
The mosaic
garden floor
The private garden of each villa is floored in small-format natural stone set in geometric patterns — a contemporary reinterpretation of the Roman garden floor tradition. The pattern is unique to each villa. No two gardens share a design. Each tessera of the estate is, in this way, irreducibly its own.
"The estate is twelve pieces. The pieces are the estate."
Every material decision at Tessera follows the mosaic principle: individual elements, individually considered, that together produce something greater than any single component. The whole is only visible from a distance. Up close, each tile is complete in itself.

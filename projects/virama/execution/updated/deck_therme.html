Vals, Graubünden, Switzerland
The building that cannot
be copied.
In 1996, Swiss architect Peter Zumthor completed a structure in a high Alpine valley using 60,000 slabs of Valser quartzite — stone quarried from the same hillside the building was cut into. It took ten years. It has no signature gesture, no hero cantilever, no atrium. It is simply, completely right. The water is just warm enough. The stone is just dark enough. The light is exactly where it should be.

The Swiss Federal Government designated the entire village of Vals a heritage site the year after it opened — not to protect the building, but to prevent anyone from trying to build beside it.
The Translation
"An architecture of presence. Not representation, not symbol. The thing itself, completely itself."
Therme in Jubilee Hills does not reference Zumthor's building in form — the climate, the site, and the brief are entirely different. It references his method: materials chosen from nearby, walls that know why they are thick, every surface having earned its position. There is no detail at Therme that was placed for a photograph. Everything was placed to be lived in.
01
Basalt
plinth
The ground floor reads as a single mineral element — quarried basalt, honed to a matte finish. It absorbs light instead of reflecting it. The building begins here, in the same material the Deccan plateau is made of.
02
Limestone
render
Upper walls in honed limestone — the same aggregate family as the thermal travertine of Roman baths, the white churches of Puglia, the cliffs of Jurassic stone. Its patina deepens over years, not decades.
03
Teak
pivot door
The entrance door is solid teak, oil-finished, three metres tall. Its weight is the weight of permanence. You do not push it — you lean into it, and it moves. This is the first thing Therme teaches you about itself.
04
Still
water
Each villa's private pool is set flush with grade — water at ground level, not raised platform level. This is not an aesthetic choice. It is an argument: that water belongs at the same elevation as the earth it occupies.
"We did not choose the materials. The site chose them for us."
Every material at Therme has a reason for being there. Not a style reason — a structural, climatic, or material reason. The result is an architecture that ages differently than buildings assembled from catalogues.

Prashashan Nagar is what parts of Jubilee Hills became when enough time had passed that they stopped needing to prove anything. The canopy grew in without instruction. The families who live here stopped talking about the address decades ago. No through traffic — which in Hyderabad in 2026 is not a feature but an inheritance, and Therme is built in its pocket. The materials do not try to stand out from the neighbourhood. They try to belong to it.

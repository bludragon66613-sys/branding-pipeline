The architect who
built with colour.
Luis Barragán won the Pritzker Prize in 1980 — architecture's highest honour — for buildings that cannot be fully understood in photographs. In his Casa Barragán (1948) and the Gilardi House (1977), colour is not applied to the walls. It is the walls. A four-metre magenta plane reaching the ceiling is not decoration — it IS the room. A pool of black water reflecting a yellow wall IS the garden. He was a devout mystic who believed that silence, solitude, and serenity were the three spiritual needs a home must answer. He called it "an emotional architecture." His buildings must be stood inside to be understood.
The Translation
"I believe in an emotional architecture. It is very important for humankind that architecture should move by its beauty."
Palomar in Jubilee Hills does not copy Barragán's Mexico — the climate, the materials, and the street are entirely different. It takes his method: that a single plane of colour, held correctly, can be the architecture itself. That a house which provides silence, solitude, and serenity is more valuable than any house assembled from premium catalogues. The magenta wall in your villa is not painted. It is structural, in the deepest sense.
01
The magenta
wall
Each villa contains one plane of Barragán magenta — four metres of pure chromatic mass. It does not frame a view. It does not receive art. It is the architecture. The room exists in relation to it, not the other way around.
02
Volcanic stone
plinth
The ground floor is set on a honed volcanic basalt plinth — the same geological family as the lava stone of Jalisco, where Barragán grew up. It absorbs light instead of reflecting it. The colour reads differently on it than on any other surface.
03
Black still
water
Each villa's private pool is finished in dark aggregate — visually black at rest. In it: the sky, the light, and the coloured walls above are reflected in slow motion. Barragán used this device at Las Arboledas. It is not a feature. It is a second room.
04
The solitary
fig tree
Every hacienda at El Palomar had one — the fig tree in the courtyard, older than the building, unmoveable. At each Palomar villa, the mature estate tree is not removed and replaced. It is designed around. The garden is secondary to it.
"Colour is not applied to the architecture. Colour is the architecture."
Every material decision at Palomar begins with how it interacts with colour and light. Stone that absorbs. Water that reflects. Plaster that holds shadow. Nothing here was chosen from a catalogue.

Palomar uses colour the way Barragán used it: not as decoration applied after the architecture is decided, but as the primary structural decision. The magenta wall in your villa is not painted — it IS the architecture. Its mass, its height, its relationship to the light entering from the west — these are the building. Everything else is secondary. Barragán also said that silence, solitude, and serenity were the three spiritual needs a home must provide. These three things are what Palomar is built to deliver.

Barragán designed for silence and separation from the city's noise. He found it in the hacienda tradition — the high wall, the inward courtyard, the shade of the single old tree. Prashashan Nagar is what Jubilee Hills becomes when it stops trying to be noticed. The canopy closed over years ago. The families here stopped explaining the address decades back. There is no through traffic — in Hyderabad in 2026, that is not a convenience, it is an inheritance. Palomar is built inside that inheritance.

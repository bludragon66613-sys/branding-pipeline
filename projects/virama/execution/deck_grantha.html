Beach Road · Singapore · Est. 1887
The hotel that
became a library.
In 1887, Regent Alfred John Bidwell designed Raffles Hotel Singapore in the colonial Palladian style. Within its rooms the Long Bar, the Tiffin Room, the Writers Bar — Somerset Maugham drank there. So did Rudyard Kipling. Joseph Conrad. The building became a cultural institution not through spectacle but through seriousness: it took the idea that a great hotel is also a great library, and it built for that idea.

Parallel to this: Grantha script. An ancient Brahmic script used across South India from the 6th to 19th century to write Sanskrit manuscripts. Still used today for ceremony. Grantha — ग्रन्थ — means manuscript. Means text. Means book.
The Translation
"The library as home. The manuscript as address. A building that holds the memory of what happened inside it."
Grantha takes both references at once. From Raffles: the colonial library as container for knowledge, for conversation, for the slow accumulation of a life's reading. From the Grantha script: the South Indian manuscript tradition as its indigenous counterpart — a civilisation that treated the written page as architecture. A home where every room is already a chapter, waiting to be written.
01
The teak
verandah
Solid Burmese teak, oiled finish — the verandah that faces inward, not outward. Every Raffles room had one. At Grantha, the verandah faces the private garden: a room between the house and the world, used for reading, for thinking, for the hours that belong to no appointment.
02
Manuscript
vault
The private library room — a dedicated volume, not a shelf on a landing. Lined in teak, with brass fittings and parchment-tone walls. Designed to hold several hundred volumes. The room that most houses do not have, and that Grantha considers essential to the brief.
03
The writers'
study
A north-facing room with a single large window and no distractions. Dark walls — rosewood render — because a writing room should feel like the inside of a thought. The desk is positioned so that the writer faces the garden, and the door is behind them. A room that closes properly.
04
Colonial
brass fitting
Every switch plate, door handle, and light fitting in solid brass — not plated, not lacquered, left to develop a patina. In twenty years the brass will be darker and more beautiful than it is today. This is a material that records the passing of time, the way a good library does.
"A house should have enough room in it for a life's worth of reading."
The Grantha brief began with this observation: that most luxury homes are designed for entertaining, not thinking. Grantha reverses the priority. The private rooms are larger. The library is load-bearing. The verandah is for sitting alone.

Grantha is built on the premise that a home should have enough room in it for a life's worth of reading. The library is not an amenity. The study is not a flex room. The verandah is not a transition space. These are the rooms the house is organised around — and everything else follows from them.

Prashashan Nagar is what parts of Jubilee Hills became when enough time had passed that the address stopped needing to announce itself. It is Hyderabad's equivalent of the Raffles neighbourhood — the part of the city that has already made its history, and now simply lives inside it. The canopy grew in without instruction. The families who live here stopped talking about the address decades ago. Grantha belongs to this quarter precisely because it is not trying to make a new statement. It is trying to belong to an old one.

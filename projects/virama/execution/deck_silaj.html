The architecture
that goes down,
not up.
Rani ki Vav was built in the 11th century by Queen Udayamati of the Chaulukya dynasty as a memorial to her husband King Bhimdev I. A stepwell is architecture that descends — seven storeys below ground, with 800 sculptures carved into its walls. The deeper you go, the cooler the air, the more intricate the carving. It is the inverse of a tower: the treasure is underground.

It was buried under silt for 700 years. The Archaeological Survey of India excavated it in 1940. It had been perfectly preserved. UNESCO World Heritage since 2014.
The Translation
"Luxury is not about height or visibility. It is about depth, interiority — the reward of going further in."
Silāj does not reference Rani ki Vav in form — the scale and the brief are entirely different. It references the logic: that the most precious things are found by going deeper, not higher. The villas at Silāj are designed for interiority — courts that draw you in, spaces that reveal themselves gradually, materials that improve with time rather than announce themselves on arrival.

The descent
corridor
The entry sequence at Silāj descends slightly below street level before rising into the court. This is deliberate. You leave the city behind. You go in before you go forward. The air changes. The temperature drops a degree. You have arrived before you have reached the door.
02
Carved stone
panel
Interior walls in hand-finished sandstone — the same material family as the Gujarat stepwell that inspired the brand. Carved detailing not as ornamentation but as evidence of time invested. A wall that could only have been made slowly. Its surface changes in different light.
03
Subterranean
cool
The Deccan basalt under Jubilee Hills is a natural temperature sink. Silāj's built form is designed to draw on this — thick walls, narrow openings at the upper levels, wider openings at grade and below. The architecture works with geology, not against it.
04
Still water
below
The private pool sits in a lowered garden — not elevated, not visible from the street. You discover it by descending. The water is below the sightline until you are at the terrace. This is the stepwell logic: the best things are found by going in, not by looking from outside.
"To build for permanence, even when no one is watching."
Rani ki Vav was carved with 800 sculptures for the dead. No one expected it to be seen again. It was preserved by depth. Silāj is built on the same logic: thoroughness is not for display. It is a property of the material itself.

Jubilee Hills sits on the Deccan Plateau — one of the oldest geological formations in the world. The basalt under Prashashan Nagar is 65 million years old. It is a natural temperature regulator, a structural foundation of exceptional density, and a material argument for building for the long term. Silāj is not in conflict with this geology. It is an extension of it. The thick walls reference the depth below. The descent logic references what was always underground.
